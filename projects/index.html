Greg Jensen: Projects

The Survey Text Formatting Helper

The Survey Text Formatting Helper (source code) is a standalone website tool with a custom text parser developed by Kayne Arcane. This allows users to easily apply a range of in-line formatting adjustments to short text passages, which just goes to show that you should be really, really careful when copying the text from a website and pasting it elsewhere.

What It's For

For the most part, our instinct when viewing text on a website is to imagine the words as if printed in a sort of digital ink. What you see, we assume, is what you get. However, websites are not at all like a page from a book, with ink that has soaked permanently into paper stock. A website is more like a live dance performance, an interpretive routine performed by your web browser that follows the choreographed instructions provided by the website. And, like any good theater venue, there's an enormous backstage area full of machinery that the audience never sees and rarely think about.

HTML and CSS are the bones of this machinery (they constitute the "markup language" that dictates how text files on a server become letter forms on your screen), and they provide a remarkably flexible framework. They are also relatively accessible: Even a small child can program a simple webpage, and while building a "modern-looking" website is quite a bit more difficult, modifying an existing page with simple HTML style adjustments, such as making a short passage underlined, or red, or blurry, is something anyone can learn to do.

The primary purpose of this tool is to help researchers who are building online surveys get a handle on how they can make in-line changes to how their survey materials are formatted. The reason for doing so, unfortunately, is that online research is rife with bad-faith actors. This might be a human participant who, 20 minutes into a survey, decides to let an AI answer a few questions on their behalf to save some time, or it might be a fully automated scammer pipeline that uses bots to fill out surveys on an industrial scale. Either way, one of the methods we can use to detect such bad-faith actors is to use in-line formatting to widen the gap between what the text of a website says and how it appears to the human eye.

The Web, But Not For Machines

Read the sentence below with your human eyes. Then, use your mouse to select the sentence, copy it, and paste it into another window.

Machines do not have eyes, so they cannot "read" websites.

Do you now see that what you copied doesn't match what your eyes see? What's going on here? To clarify, here's the same text, with a slight modification to how the text is formatted.

Machines do not have eyes, so they cannot "read" websites.

You should now see that there are extra letters in a smaller font size. Each of these bits of text is embedded in a <span> tag that applies the CSS style "font-size: 50%", thereby shrinking how large the letters appear on your screen. The main difference between the two examples above is that the first instead sets the font size of those extra sections to 0%. In other words, the reason why what your eyes see and what your computer copies in that first example is that it has hidden characters intermingled with its visible characters.

Let's consider another example. Try selecting the sentence below, copying it, and pasting it elsewhere.

Thaat whiich dooees noot kiill uus maakees uus stroangeer.

While selecting this text, you may have noticed something suspicious (what's with all the vowels?!), but look closely as the text you pasted. Does it match the text above?

This is a more elaborate trick, and is likely to confound at least some LLMs that look at its source code, at least for the time being. It relies on three factors: (1) hidden "font-size: 0%" text, (2) text that has been rendered "non-selectable" so your web browser doesn't let you highlight it in the first place, and (3) the "incorrect" quotation being much, much more famous than the variant that appears to the naked eye. That last detail is deceptively important: Incorrect interpretations are much more appealing to LLMs if they are also very popular. Food for thought.

The above formatting tricks are annoying to write from scratch in HTML, and are likely to be intimidating to someone unfamiliar with programming generally. The Survey Text Formatting Helper, linked above, provides assistance in applying these and many other bits of in-line HTML formatting to widen the gap between what the eye sees and what copying and pasting produces. The hope is that this will help people make use of this sort of mischief when developing materials for online research, or for presentation online in general.

Making text both more and less than it seems may feel like a hostile design philosophy, and it arguably is. The question is: To whom is it hostile? Provided you, as a web designer, are causing all the text that humans would need to see to be rendered, it's not hostile to humans. If, however, the parties most inconvenienced by this are bots, web crawlers, plagiarists, and other scoundrels, then perhaps some hostility is warranted.

On June 3rd, 2026, Matthew Prince (the CEO of content delivery networking titan Cloudflare) announced that automated queries (i.e., "bots") had overtaken human queries and were a majority of the overall volume of Internet traffic. If a web server receives a request, then all else being equal, it's now more likely to come from a machine than from a person. In light of this, we should perhaps begin to be more deliberate about how we tailor information that is delivered via the web. We are not obligated to build websites where everything is as it appears to the (biological) naked eye.

The Arms Race

This tool is a drop in the bucket when it comes to staring down the vast array of industries that concern themselves with treating the Internet like a source of training data. However, within the relatively narrow confined of surveys designed for online research, it is my position that engineering more mischief into task designs remains at least a somewhat effective way to combat some proportion of the bad actors who might sign up to participate. However, if any particular technique becomes popular enough to be "standard," then bad-faith actors (and possibly even tech companies) will start to engineer their way around it.

The best bet for the time being is for every researching using tools like these to use them a little differently. The more ways in which people try to defend themselves from bad-faith actors, the harder it will be for those actors to devise one-size-fits-all solutions. So, play with the tool and try out a bunch of different things. Try messing with blur and opacity. Venture, if you dare, into the tricksy world of reversed text. Mix and match these style modifications. Consider incorporating HTML trickery into other, unrelated tools for measuring attention and authenticity. I cannot guarantee that every technique will be effective at combating bots, but I think the more diverse our efforts in general, the better a collective defense we will be able to mount.

Greg Jensen, June 9th, 2026
